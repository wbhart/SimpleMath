Binomial Coefficients

The integer coefficients of $(x + y)^n$ are called the binomial coefficients. We denote the coefficient of $x^{n - i}y^i$ by $\binom{n}{i}$. For $0 \leq i \leq n$ we have $$\binom{n}{i} = \binom{n}{n - i}.$$ We have $(x + y)^n = (y + x)^n$. But the coefficient of $x^{n - i}y^i$ is $\binom{n}{i}$ on the left and $\binom{n}{n - i}$ on the right. The binomial coefficients are characterised by
  • For all $n \geq 0$, $\binom{n}{0} = \binom{n}{n} = 1$
  • For $n > 1$ and $0 < i < n$, $\binom{n}{i} = \binom{n - 1}{i - 1} + \binom{n - 1}{i}$.
 For $n = 0$ the only binomial coefficient is $\binom{0}{0}$. It corresponds to the single coefficient in $(x + y)^0$, which is $1$.

For all $n$ we have that $\binom{n}{0}$ is the coefficient of $x^n$ in $(x + y)^n$, i.e. $1$. By the previous theorem we also have $\binom{n}{n} = 1$. All other binomial coefficients are of the form $\binom{n}{i}$ for $n > 1$ and $0 < i < n$. The theorem defines these inductively in terms of binomial coefficients of the form $\binom{n - 1}{j}$. Thus it suffices to show that the second relation of the theorem actually holds for binomial coefficients.

We have that $(x + y)^n = (x + y)^{n-1}(x + y)$. The $x^{n - i}y^i$ term is $x$ times the term $\binom{n - 1}{i}x^{n - i - 1}y^i$ plus $y$ times the term $\binom{n - 1}{i - 1}x^{n - i}y^{i - 1}$. Thus, $\binom{n}{i}$, the coefficient of $x^{n - i}{y^i}$ is $\binom{n - 1}{i} + \binom{n - 1}{i - 1}$. For $n \geq 0$ and $0 \leq i \leq n$ we have that $$\binom{n}{i} = \frac{n!}{(n - i)!i!}.$$ Let us write $C(n, i) = \frac{n!}{(n - i)!i!}$. It suffices to show that $C(n, i)$ satisfies the conditions of the previous theorem since these completely characterise the binomial coefficients.

We have that for all $n$, $C(n, 0) = \frac{n!}{n!0!} = 1$. Similarly, $C(n, n) = \frac{n!}{0!n!} = 1$.

Finally $$C(n - 1, i - 1) + C(n - 1, i) = \frac{(n - 1)!}{(n - i)!(i - 1)!} + \frac{(n - 1)!}{(n - i - 1)!i!}$$ $$= \frac{(n - 1)!(i + (n - i))}{(n - i)!i!} = \frac{n!}{(n - i)!i!} = C(n, i).$$ Thus we must have $C(n, i) = \binom{n}{i}$. For $n \geq 0$ we have $$\sum_{i = 0}^n \binom{n}{i} = 2^n.$$ We expand out the expression $(x + y)^n$ with $x = 1$ and $y = 1$. This yields $$\sum_{i = 0}^n \binom{n}{i} = (1 + 1)^n = 2^n.$$ For $n \geq 0$ we have $$\sum_{i = 0}^n 2^i\binom{n}{i} = 3^n.$$ We expand out the expression $(x + y)^n$ with $x = 2$ and $y = 1$. This yields $$\sum_{i = 0}^n 2^i\binom{n}{i} = (2 + 1)^n = 3^n.$$ For $n \geq 1$ we have $$\sum_{i = 0}^n (-1)^i\binom{n}{i} = 0.$$ We expand out the expression $(x + y)^n$ with $x = -1$ and $y = 1$. This yields $$\sum_{i = 0}^n (-1)^i\binom{n}{i} = (-1 + 1)^n = 0,$$ when $n > 0$. For $n \geq 0$ we have $$\sum_{i = 0}^n \binom{n}{i} = n2^{n - 1}.$$ We differentiate the expression $$(x + 1)^n = \sum_{i = 0}^n \binom{n}{i}x^i.$$ This yields $$n(x + 1)^{n - 1} = \sum_{i = 1}^n i\binom{n}{i}x^{i - 1}.$$ Substituting $x = 1$ into the expression yields $$n2^{n - 1} = \sum_{i = 1}^n i\binom{n}{i} = \sum_{i = 0}^n i\binom{n}{i}.$$ For every natural number $n$ and $0 < i \leq n$ we have $$\frac{n - 2i}{n}\binom{n}{i} = \binom{n - 1}{i} - \binom{n - 1}{i - 1}.$$ We have $$\binom{n - 1}{i} - \binom{n - 1}{i - 1} = \frac{n - i}{n}\binom{n}{i} - \frac{i}{n}\binom{n}{i} = \frac{n - 2i}{n}\binom{n}{i}.$$ For every $n, i, j \geq 0$ with $0 \leq i + j \leq n$ we have $$\binom{n}{i}\binom{n - i}{j} = \binom{n}{j}\binom{n - j}{i}.$$ By symmetry, we may suppose without loss of generality that $i \geq j$. We have $$\binom{n}{i}\binom{n - i}{j} = \frac{n!}{i!(n - i)!}\frac{(n - i)!}{j!(n - i - j)!} = \frac{n!}{i!}\frac{1}{j!(n - i - j)!}$$ $$= \frac{n!}{i!(n - j)!}\frac{(n - j)!}{j!(n - i - j)!} = \frac{n!}{j!(n - j)!}\frac{(n - j)!}{i!(n - i - j)!} = \binom{n}{j}\binom{n - j}{i}.$$ (Vandermonde) For $n \geq m \geq 0$ and $k + m \leq m$ with $k \geq 0$, we have $$\sum_{i = 0}^k \binom{m}{i}\binom{n - m}{k - i} = \binom{n}{k}.$$ Consider the coefficient of $x^k$ in $(1 + x)^n = (1 + x)^m(1 + x)^{n - m}$. On the left side we have $\binom{n}{k}$. On the right side we have $\sum_{i = 0}^k \binom{m}{i}\binom{n - m}{k - i}$. For $n \geq 0$ we have $$\sum_{i = 0}^n \binom{n}{i}^2 = \binom{2n}{n}.$$ In the previous theorem, replace $n$ with $2n$, $k$ with $n$ and $m$ with $n$. This yields $$\sum_{i = 0}^n \binom{n}{i}\binom{n}{n - i} = \binom{2n}{n}.$$ The result follows on noting that $\binom{n}{i} = \binom{n}{n - i}$. Let $F(1) = 1$, $F(2) = 1$ and $F(i + 1) = F(i) + F(i - 1)$ for all $i \geq 2$. The sequence of numbers $F(1) = 1$, $F(2) = 1$, $F(3) = 2$, $F(4) = 3$, $F(5) = 5$, $\ldots$ is called the Fibonacci sequence. For $n \geq 0$ we have $$\sum_{i = 0}^{\lfloor \frac{n}{2} \rfloor} \binom{n - i}{i} = F(n + 1).$$ We proceed by induction. For $n = 0, 1$ the left hand is $1$, and indeed $F(1) = F(2) = 1$.

Assume the result is true for $F(1)$, $F(2)$, $\ldots$, $F(n)$. Then we have $$F(n + 1) = F(n) + F(n - 1) = \sum_{i = 0}^{\lfloor \frac{n - 2}{2} \rfloor} \binom{n - 2 - i}{i} + \sum_{i = 0}^{\lfloor \frac{n - 1}{2} \rfloor} \binom{n - 1 - i}{i}.$$ But $\binom{n - 1 - i}{i} + \binom{n - 2 - (i - 1)}{i - 1} = \binom{n - i}{i}$. So if $n - 1$ is even, the value above is $$1 + \sum_{i = 1}^{\lfloor \frac{n - 1}{2} \rfloor} \binom{n - i}{i} = \sum_{i = 0}^{\lfloor \frac{n}{2} \rfloor} \binom{n - i}{i}.$$ Similarly, if $n - 1$ is odd, the value is $$1 + \binom{\frac{n - 2}{2}}{\frac{n - 2}{2}} + \sum_{i = 1}^{\lfloor \frac{n - 1}{2} \rfloor} \binom{n - i}{i} = \sum_{i = 0}^{\lfloor \frac{n}{2} \rfloor} \binom{n - i}{i}.$$ If $f(x) = \sum_{i = 0}^n a_ix^i$ and $g(x) = \sum_{i = 0}^n b_ix^i$ then $\sum_{i = 0}^n _ib_i$ is the constant term of $f(x)g(x^{-1})$. We have $f(x)g(x^{-1}) = (\sum_{i = 0}^n a_ix^i)(sum_{j = 0}^n b_jx^{-j})$. The constant term consists of all the product terms with $i = j$, i.e. $\sum_{i = 0}^n a_ib_i$. For $n \geq 0$ we have $$\sum_{i = 0}^2n (-1)^i \binom{2n}{i}^2 = (-1)^n \binom{2n}{n}.$$ We apply the previous theorem with $f(x) = \sum_{i = 0}^{2n} (-1)^i\binom{2n}{i}x^i$ and $g(x) = \sum_{i = 0}^{2n} \binom{2n}{i}x^i$ (replacing $n$ with $2n$ in the theorem). The left hand side is then the constant term in $f(x)g(x^{-1})$. But we see $f(x) = $(x - 1)^{2n}$ and $g(x) = (x + 1)^{2n}$. Thus $f(x)g(x^{-1})$ is $(x - 1)^2n (x^{-1} + 1)^{2n}$.

The constant term in this expression is the coefficient of $x^{2n}$ in $$x^{2n}f(x)g(x^{-1}) = (x - 1)^2n (x + 1)^{2n} = (x^2 - 1)^{2n},$$ which is $(-1)^n \binom{2n}{n}$.
